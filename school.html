Introduction to Card Counting

Card counting is a strategy used in blackjack to determine when the player has a statistical advantage over the house. By keeping track of the cards dealt, card counters can make more informed decisions on when to hit, stand, double down, or split, as well as adjust their betting strategies. In this guide, you'll learn the basics of card counting and the most popular card counting systems.

The Basics of Card Counting

Card counting is based on the principle that high-value cards (10s and face cards) benefit the player, while low-value cards (2s through 6s) benefit the dealer. The higher the count, the more high-value cards remain in the deck, and the greater the player's advantage. Here are the basic steps to card counting:

  1. Assign a point value to each card (based on the counting system).
  2. Keep a running count of the cards as they are dealt.
  3. Convert the running count to the true count by dividing by the number of decks remaining.
  4. Adjust your bet size and playing decisions based on the true count.

Popular Card Counting Systems

There are several card counting systems, each with its own set of rules and assigned point values. The most popular system is the Hi-Lo System.

1. Hi-Lo System

The Hi-Lo System is a balanced card counting system, which means that if you were to count down an entire deck, you would end with a count of 0. The point values for the Hi-Lo System are:

  • 2-6: +1
  • 7-9: 0
  • 10-Ace: -1

Basic Strategy

History of Basic Strategy in Blackjack

The history of basic strategy in Blackjack dates back to the 1950s when a group of mathematicians and statisticians began studying the game in-depth. The most notable contributors to this work were Roger Baldwin, Wilbert Cantey, Herbert Maisel, and James McDermott, who together published a paper called The Optimum Strategy in Blackjack in the Journal of the American Statistical Association in 1956. This paper laid the foundation for what we now know as basic strategy.

The development of basic strategy involved calculating the probabilities of various outcomes for each possible combination of player hands and dealer upcards. This required a considerable amount of computational work, especially considering that the work was done before the widespread availability of computers. The team used calculators, pencil, and paper to solve the complex statistical problems involved in determining the optimal plays.

What is Basic Strategy?

Basic strategy is a set of rules for playing Blackjack, based on the statistical probabilities of the game. It tells players the expectation maximizing action to take (hit, stand, double down, split, or surrender) in any given situation, based on their hand and the dealer's upcard. By following basic strategy, players can minimize the house edge and increase their chances of winning in the long run.

The importance of basic strategy lies in its ability to improve a player's odds in a game that is generally designed to favor the house. By adhering to basic strategy, players can reduce the house edge to as low as 0.5% in some variations of the game, making it one of the best games in the casino for players in terms of odds. Futhermore, for card counters memorizing basic strategy is important because you don't want to have to think about your hit/stand decisions as your mind will be busy counting cards.

It's essential to note that while basic strategy offers the best statistical approach to the game, it doesn't guarantee a win in every session or even eliminate the house edge entirely. It is a long-term strategy that improves the player's chances of winning over time.

Evolution of Basic Strategy

Since the inception of basic strategy, several researchers, card-counting experts, and professional gamblers have refined and expanded upon the work of Baldwin and his colleagues. Edward O. Thorp's book "Beat the Dealer," published in 1962, was a groundbreaking work that not only promoted basic strategy but also introduced card counting as a viable method to gain an advantage over the house. Today, basic strategy remains a fundamental concept for anyone looking to improve their Blackjack skills and increase their chances of success at the game.

How to read a basic strategy chart

A Blackjack basic strategy chart is a table that shows the optimal action (hit, stand, double down, split, or surrender) a player should take based on their hand and the dealer's upcard. The chart is designed to help you minimize the house edge and maximize your chances of winning in the long run. Here's how to read a basic strategy chart:

  1. Find your hand: The rows in a basic strategy chart typically represent the player's hand. Hands are usually organized into three categories: hard totals (no Ace or Ace counts as 1), soft totals (Ace counts as 11), and pairs. Locate the row that corresponds to your hand.
  2. Find the dealer's upcard: The columns in a basic strategy chart represent the dealer's upcard, usually ranging from 2 to Ace. Find the column that corresponds to the dealer's upcard.
  3. Determine the optimal action: Once you've found the row for your hand and the column for the dealer's upcard, look at the cell where they intersect. This cell will tell you the best action to take, usually abbreviated as follows:
    • H: Hit
    • S: Stand
    • D: Double down (if not allowed, follow the "Hit" action)
    • P: Split (if not allowed or out of re-splits, follow the "Hit" or "Stand" action, depending on the specific chart)
    • Rh: Surrender if allowed, otherwise hit (usually found in charts for games that allow surrender)

Follow the action indicated in the chart to maximize your chances of winning in the long run. Remember that the basic strategy may vary slightly depending on the specific game rules, such as the number of decks, whether the dealer hits or stands on soft 17, and whether you are allowed to double after you split. Make sure you use a chart tailored to the rules of the game you're playing.

The True Count and Index Plays

Converting to the true count and deviating from basic strategy based on the true count, also known as index plays, is a more advanced technique used in card counting systems to gain a better understanding of the remaining cards in the deck and adjust your strategy accordingly. This technique is typically used in combination with basic strategy and card counting to gain an edge over the house.

To convert the running count into the true count, you need to consider the number of decks remaining in the shoe. The true count is calculated by dividing the running count by the approximate number of decks left to be dealt. Here's the formula:

True Count = Running Count / Number of Decks Remaining

For example, if your running count is +10 and you estimate there are 5 decks remaining in the shoe, the true count would be +2:

True Count = +10 / 5 = +2

Index plays refer to specific situations in which you deviate from basic strategy based on the true count. These deviations are pre-calculated and tested to provide a more accurate assessment of the best action to take, given the composition of the remaining cards in the deck. They help you take advantage of favorable situations and avoid unfavorable ones.

Each card counting system has its own set of index plays. You will need to learn and memorize these deviations to use them effectively. For example, in the Hi-Lo counting system, an index play might dictate that you should stand with a hard 16 against the dealer's 10 when the true count is greater than or equal to +1. In this case, you would deviate from basic strategy (which recommends hitting) and stand instead.

Remember that learning index plays and incorporating them into your game requires practice and experience. Make sure you have a solid understanding of basic strategy and card counting before attempting to use index plays at the table.

Optimal Betting

The Kelly Criterion is a popular betting system used by gamblers to determine the optimal size of a bet based on their advantage in a given wager. It aims to maximize the long-term growth of your bankroll while minimizing the risk of losing it. The formula for the Kelly Criterion is:

Fraction to bet = (Edge / Odds) or (bp - q) / b

Where:

  • b = odds received on the wager
  • p = probability of winning
  • q = probability of losing (1 - p)

In the context of card counting in Blackjack, the edge represents your advantage over the house, and the odds are generally even money (1:1). Betting 70% of your advantage * bankroll (e.g., 1% edge on a $10,000 bankroll = $70 bet) is a simplified version of the Kelly Criterion, taking into account that blackjack is only a pseudo even money game.

There are several reasons why this betting strategy is considered optimal:

  1. Maximizes bankroll growth: The Kelly Criterion allows you to make the most of your advantage while minimizing the risk of losing your entire bankroll. By betting a proportion of your bankroll based on your edge, you can maximize your long-term profits.
  2. Adjusts bet size based on advantage: When your advantage is higher, the Kelly Criterion recommends increasing your bet size, and when your advantage is lower, it recommends decreasing your bet size. This ensures that you are always betting an amount proportional to your advantage, which helps increase your bankroll more quickly.
  3. Reduces the risk of ruin: Since the Kelly Criterion calculates the optimal bet size based on your advantage, it helps you avoid overbetting and significantly reduces the risk of losing your entire bankroll. This is especially important in games like Blackjack, where the player's advantage can change frequently.
  4. Adapts to your current bankroll: The Kelly Criterion is a proportional betting system, meaning that your bet size will increase as your bankroll grows and decrease as your bankroll shrinks. This ensures that you are always betting an appropriate amount based on your current financial situation.

While the Kelly Criterion is an effective betting strategy, it's important to remember that it requires precise calculations and a solid understanding of your advantage in a given game. Additionally, implementing the Kelly Criterion in a casino environment can be challenging due to bet limits and other practical factors. However, when used correctly, it can significantly increase your bankroll over time.
